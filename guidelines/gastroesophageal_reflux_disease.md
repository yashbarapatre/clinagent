# ACG Clinical Guideline: Gastroesophageal Reflux Disease (GERD) Management

## 1. Diagnostic Criteria & Evaluation
- **Clinical Diagnosis**: 
  - Presumptive diagnosis is made based on typical symptoms of heartburn and/or acid regurgitation occurring 2 or more times per week.
  - An empiric 8-week trial of Proton Pump Inhibitor (PPI) once daily is diagnostic and therapeutic for typical symptoms.
- **Alarm Symptoms (Indications for urgent Upper Endoscopy / EGD)**:
  - Dysphagia (difficulty swallowing) or Odynophagia (painful swallowing).
  - Unexplained weight loss.
  - Recurrent vomiting.
  - Gastrointestinal bleeding (hematemesis, melena) or iron-deficiency anemia.
- **Endoscopy Guidance**:
  - Perform EGD in patients with alarm symptoms or those at high risk for Barrett's Esophagus (e.g., males > 50 with chronic symptoms).

## 2. Guideline-Directed Pharmacotherapy (PPIs & H2RAs)
Proton Pump Inhibitors (PPIs) are the most effective therapy for GERD symptom resolution and healing of erosive esophagitis.
- **Initiation (First-Line Therapy)**:
  - Standard dose PPI once daily for an 8-week course.
  - **Omeprazole**: 20 mg PO daily.
  - **Pantoprazole**: 40 mg PO daily.
  - **Esomeprazole**: 20 - 40 mg PO daily.
- **Administration Instructions (CRITICAL)**:
  - PPIs must be taken 30-60 minutes before the first meal of the day (usually breakfast) rather than at bedtime, to optimize parietal cell proton-pump block.
- **Maintenance / Step-Down Therapy**:
  - After 8 weeks, attempt to discontinue or step-down to the lowest effective dose of PPI or to a Histamine-2 Receptor Antagonist (H2RA, e.g., Famotidine 20 mg BID) for patients with resolved symptoms.
  - For long-term PPI therapy, monitor for potential complications (B12 deficiency, hypomagnesemia, osteoporosis, nephritis, and C. diff-associated diarrhea).

## 3. Lifestyle and Dietary Modifications
Clinicians must counsel all GERD patients on lifestyle modifications:
- Weight loss if overweight or obese.
- Avoid meals/snacks within 2-3 hours of lying down or sleeping.
- Elevate the head of the bed (by 6-8 inches) or use a wedge pillow for nighttime symptoms.
- Avoid trigger foods (caffeine, chocolate, alcohol, peppermint, citrus, spicy foods).
- Smoking cessation.

## 4. Discharge & Follow-up Criteria
- Symptom stability: Reflux symptoms are adequately controlled on oral medical therapy (PPI/H2RA).
- Absence of uncontrolled alarm symptoms (no active GI bleeding, patient tolerating solid/liquid oral intake).
- Patient demonstrates understanding of proper PPI administration timing and lifestyle modifications.
- Outpatient primary care or gastroenterology follow-up scheduled in 4-8 weeks to evaluate for PPI safety, efficacy, and possible step-down.
